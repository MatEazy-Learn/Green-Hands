Sudan's Paradox

A nation rich in gold struggles with severe water shortages amid devastating conflict

Humanitarian Crisis

Sudan, a country blessed with abundant gold reserves, faces a severe water crisis that deeply affects its economy, society, and daily lifeâ€”especially amidst a war that has dragged on for over a year. While gold exports generate billions, millions struggle to access clean water, creating a stark contrast between national wealth and human suffering.

Sudan's Golden Wealth

In the first quarter of this year alone, Sudan exported over 7.626 tons of gold, valued at $428 million. Projections suggest that by the end of the year, gold exports could reach $2.182 billion.

The country sits on some of Africa's richest gold deposits, with mining operations ranging from large industrial sites to small-scale artisanal mines that employ thousands of Sudanese.

Despite this mineral wealth, government mismanagement and corruption mean little of this revenue reaches basic services like water infrastructure.

The Devastating Water Crisis

Children walking long distances to fetch water in Darfur. (Photo credit: AFP)

In the displacement camp of Sortony in North Darfur, home to over 65,000 people, Issa, a father of several children, shares his daily struggle: "Since the start of the war in April 2023, two of my children walk 14 kilometers each day just to fetch water."

Darfur, a vast region scarred by conflict, hosts the majority of Sudan's displaced people. The ongoing shortage of water only adds to the daily hardships faced by millions.

Even before the war, 26% of Sudanese had to walk over 50 minutes to access clean water (OCHA), exposing women to significant security risks.

Root Causes of the Water Crisis

War & Conflict

Humanitarian workers fled after fighting began, abandoning water projects

Climate Change

East Africa's worst drought in 40 years with 5 failed rainy seasons

Fuel Shortages

Wells can't operate without fuel for pumps in remote areas

Human Impact

Daily Struggles in Shaqra Village

In the village of Shaqra in Darfur, which shelters around 40,000 displaced individuals, residents queue for hours under the burning sun, waiting their turn at the scarce water sources. Lines can stretch over 300 meters.

El Fasher Crisis

The humanitarian situation is especially dire in El Fasher, the capital of North Darfur State, home to 1.5 million people. Since May, heavy fighting has isolated the city, creating severe shortages of food and drinking water.

A European diplomat familiar with the situation noted: "Even if water is available underground, without fuel or a functioning pump, it's out of reach. Water often lies more than 60 meters deep, making manual pumping virtually impossible."

Potential Solutions

Immediate Needs

  • Emergency fuel supplies to operate existing wells and pumps

  • Humanitarian corridors to deliver water purification supplies

  • Mobile water treatment units for displacement camps

Long-Term Solutions

  • Investment in solar-powered water pumps for sustainability

  • Better governance of gold revenues to fund infrastructure

  • Climate adaptation programs for water management

International organizations are calling for increased funding and safe access for humanitarian workers to address both immediate needs and long-term water security solutions.
