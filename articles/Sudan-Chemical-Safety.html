Comprehensive Guide for Sudanese Civilians

Prevention and Response to Chemical Weapons and Toxic Gases

Actionable advice from international guidelines to help civilians mitigate exposure, respond effectively, and protect their communities during the ongoing conflict.

Preparedness During Attack Decontamination Medical Response Emergency Contacts

The ongoing conflict in Sudan has raised grave concerns about the use of chemical weapons, including mustard gas, which poses severe health and environmental risks. This report synthesizes actionable advice from international guidelines and scientific research to help civilians mitigate exposure, respond effectively, and protect their communities.

Mustard gas exposure can cause delayed symptoms (2-24 hours after exposure) including skin blistering, eye damage, and respiratory distress.

1. Preparedness: Before Exposure

Education and Awareness

  • Learn to recognize signs of chemical attacks: unusual odors (garlic, mustard), sudden eye/skin irritation, or unexplained respiratory distress
  • Participate in community training sessions to identify safe shelters and evacuation routes
  • Memorize the smell of mustard gas (similar to garlic, onions, or horseradish)

Emergency Kits

  • Prepare a kit containing waterproof gloves, plastic sheeting, duct tape, soap, clean water, and sealed food supplies
  • Include a battery-powered radio to receive updates from authorities
  • Add plastic goggles and activated charcoal (if available) for emergency protection

Protective Measures

Clothing Protection

  • Use thick plastic clothing or rubber materials if available
  • Ordinary fabrics offer limited protection against mustard gas
  • Multiple layers can provide some additional protection

Water & Food Safety

  • Store clean water in sealed containers to avoid contamination
  • Keep food in airtight containers or plastic bags
  • Identify alternative water sources in advance

2. During a Chemical Attack: Immediate Actions

Sheltering In Place

  • Move indoors immediately. Seal windows, doors, and vents with plastic sheets, duct tape, or wet towels
  • Avoid basements or low-lying areas, as mustard gas is heavier than air
  • Turn off ventilation systems to prevent gas circulation

If Outdoors

  • Move upwind and seek elevated terrain immediately
  • Cover your nose and mouth with a cloth (wet if possible) to reduce inhalation
  • Do not run; slow breathing to minimize toxin intake

Protecting Vulnerable Groups

  • Prioritize children, the elderly, and pregnant individuals for sheltering
  • Infants are especially vulnerable - cover carriers with plastic sheeting
  • Those with respiratory conditions should be given priority for medical attention

3. Decontamination Procedures

Removing Contaminated Clothing

  • Cut off clothing (do not pull over the head) to avoid contact with eyes/nose
  • Place contaminated items in sealed plastic bags if possible
  • Use gloves to handle contaminated materials
  • Remove contact lenses immediately if present

Skin and Eye Washing

  • Rinse exposed skin with soap and water for at least 90 seconds
  • For eyes, flush with clean water for 10–15 minutes (tilt head so water runs from inner to outer eye)
  • Avoid using bleach or harsh chemicals unless directed (0.5% diluted bleach may be used for skin decontamination in emergencies)
  • Do not rub affected areas as this spreads contamination

Environmental Cleanup

Contamination Areas

  • Avoid contact with contaminated soil or water
  • Mustard gas can persist for weeks in cold climates
  • Mark contaminated areas clearly to warn others

Reporting & Professional Help

  • Report contaminated areas to local authorities immediately
  • Wait for professional decontamination teams
  • Do not attempt large-scale decontamination without proper equipment

4. Medical Response and Long-Term Care

Immediate Medical Attention

  • Seek help even if symptoms are delayed (e.g., blistering or respiratory issues may appear hours after exposure)
  • Do not induce vomiting if mustard gas is ingested; administer milk if available
  • Remove contact lenses immediately if present
  • Inform medical personnel about the exposure details (duration, type of chemical if known)

Long-Term Health Monitoring

  • Monitor for chronic conditions: respiratory diseases, skin cancer, or vision loss
  • Advocate for community health programs to track late-onset symptoms
  • Regular eye exams for those with eye exposure
  • Psychological support for trauma and anxiety related to the attack

Mustard Gas Symptoms Timeline

Time After Exposure Symptoms Recommended Action
0-2 hours Mild skin/eye irritation, possible nausea Begin decontamination immediately
2-8 hours Reddening of skin, eye pain, possible blisters Seek medical attention
8-24 hours Severe skin blistering, eye swelling, respiratory distress Hospitalization likely needed
Days to Years Possible long-term respiratory issues, skin cancer risk, eye damage Regular medical monitoring

5. Community and Advocacy Measures

Environmental Safety

  • Avoid returning to contaminated zones until cleared by experts
  • Mustard gas can linger in soil for years in cold climates
  • Demand accountability for chemical weapon use through local and international channels
  • Organize community monitoring of potentially affected areas

Psychological Support

  • Address trauma and anxiety through community-led counseling initiatives
  • Chemical attacks often cause lasting psychological harm
  • Special attention needed for children who may develop PTSD
  • Create support groups for survivors and affected families

Conclusion

The threat of chemical weapons demands vigilance, preparedness, and collective action. By adhering to these guidelines, Sudanese civilians can reduce risks, protect their families, and advocate for a safer environment. Share this information widely and stay informed through trusted sources.
