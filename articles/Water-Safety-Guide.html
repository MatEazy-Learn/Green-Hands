Identifying Safe Drinking Water

Learn to distinguish between clean water and water contaminated with chlorine or cholera bacteria through simple observation and testing.

Safe Water Signs Safety Actions

Characteristics of Clean, Potable Water

Safe drinking water should meet these criteria to ensure it's free from harmful contaminants

Safe Drinking Water

  • Appearance

    Clear, colorless, with no floating particles or sediment

  • Smell

    Odorless with no chemical or organic odors

  • Taste

    Neutral taste with no chemical or metallic flavors

  • Source

    Treated municipal supply, sealed bottles, or properly filtered/boiled water

Safety Confirmation

Test Strips

Use EPA-approved test strips to check pH, chlorine, and nitrate levels. All parameters should be within safe limits.

Water Reports

Municipal water systems provide annual water quality reports showing compliance with safety standards.

Identifying Water Contaminants

Learn to recognize the signs of chlorine overexposure and cholera contamination

Chlorine-Contaminated Water

  • Appearance

    Usually clear but may have faint yellow/green tint at high concentrations

  • Smell

    Strong bleach-like odor even at low levels

  • Taste

    Chemical, bitter, or "swimming pool" flavor

  • Physical Effects

    Eye/skin irritation when bathing, nausea or throat burning if ingested

Verification Tests

  • Test strips detect chlorine levels >4 mg/L (EPA limit is 4 ppm)
  • DIY Test: Add lemon juice; if bleach smell fades, chlorine is present

Cholera-Contaminated Water

  • Appearance

    Often murky or cloudy due to fecal/organic matter (but can be clear!)

  • Smell

    Earthy, musty, or sewage-like odor (but can be odorless)

  • Taste

    May taste earthy or normal (Vibrio cholerae has no taste)

  • Key Risk Indicators

    Shallow wells, rivers, or areas with poor sanitation/floods

Health Symptoms (if ingested)

  • Severe watery diarrhea ("rice-water stools") within 12h–5 days
  • Rapid dehydration (sunken eyes, dry mouth, extreme thirst)

Critical Differences Summary

Quick reference table to compare water characteristics

Feature Clean Water Chlorine-Contaminated Cholera-Contaminated
Smell None Bleach-like Musty/sewage (or none)
Taste Neutral Bitter/chemical Earthy (or normal)
Appearance Clear Clear/yellow-green tint Cloudy, particles
Onset of Illness None Minutes-hours (burning) Hours-days (diarrhea)
Test Method EPA strip (all normal) Chlorine test strip Boil/filter + lab test

Important Notes

  • Chlorine is often used safely in water treatment (0.2–4 ppm). Only levels >4 ppm pose health risks.
  • Cholera-contaminated water shows no visible signs in many cases—prioritize source safety (e.g., avoid floodwater).
  • Always store water in clean, covered containers to prevent contamination.

Action Steps for Water Safety

What to do when you suspect water contamination

Boil Water

Bring water to a rolling boil for at least 1 minute (kills cholera and other pathogens).

  • Effective against biological contaminants
  • Does not remove chemical contaminants

Use Certified Filters

NSF/EPA-certified filters (e.g., reverse osmosis) remove many contaminants.

  • Look for filters that remove bacteria and chemicals
  • Regularly maintain and replace filter cartridges

Chemical Disinfection

Chlorine tablets or drops can disinfect water when boiling isn't possible.

  • Follow instructions carefully to avoid over-chlorination
  • Let treated water stand for 30 minutes before drinking

Seek Help If...

Chemical Contamination

Contact local environmental agency if water smells/tastes chemical

Cholera Symptoms

Seek medical care immediately for diarrhea/dehydration (cholera is treatable)
