Understanding Sudan's Seismic Landscape

Sudan, situated in a tectonically complex region of Northeast Africa, faces a low to moderate earthquake hazard, with a 2% probability of potentially damaging shaking in any given area over the next 50 years.

While large-magnitude earthquakes are rare, historical data reveals notable events, such as the 6.2-magnitude quake near Kassala in 1915 and a 4.7-magnitude tremor east of Port Sudan in July 2024.

Over the past decade, Sudan has experienced an average of two earthquakes annually with magnitudes above 4.0, primarily clustered near its eastern border with Ethiopia and Eritrea. These events, though typically minor, underscore the need for preparedness in a country already grappling with conflict, displacement, and strained infrastructure.

Key Vulnerabilities in Sudan

Multiple factors combine to make Sudan particularly vulnerable to earthquake impacts

Urbanization & Infrastructure

Rapid, unplanned urbanization in cities like Khartoum and Port Sudan has led to poorly constructed buildings lacking seismic-resistant design. Limited enforcement of building codes and insufficient technical expertise exacerbate the problem.

Humanitarian Crises

Ongoing conflict and displacement have left over 12.6 million people internally displaced or living in fragile shelters, increasing their exposure to earthquake-related risks such as collapsing structures and landslides.

Critical Infrastructure

Dams, such as the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam (GERD) near Sudan's border, highlight regional vulnerabilities. While designed to withstand seismic activity, hypothetical failures could devastate Sudan's low-lying Nile Basin.

Strategies for Mitigating Earthquake Risks

A multi-faceted approach integrating technical, policy, and community-based interventions

1. Strengthening Building Standards

Sudan lacks comprehensive building regulations tailored to earthquake hazards. Adopting and enforcing standards from low-seismic-risk regions can reduce structural vulnerabilities. Training engineers in earthquake-resistant design is critical, with potential collaborations with international institutions.

  • Enforce seismic-resistant building codes
  • Invest in technical expertise through international partnerships
  • Conduct soil and site assessments before construction

2. Early Warning Systems & Preparedness

Raising awareness about earthquake risks through local campaigns can empower residents to respond effectively. Implementing sensor-based systems to detect tremors and issue alerts would provide critical seconds for evacuation.

  • Community education programs in schools and displacement camps
  • Installation of early warning infrastructure
  • Regular earthquake drills and response training

3. Integrating DRR into Humanitarian Efforts

Sudan's ongoing humanitarian crises demand that earthquake preparedness be woven into broader disaster response plans. Upgrading temporary shelters in displacement camps to withstand seismic shocks is essential given their exposure to collapsing structures.

  • Reinforce displacement camps with seismic-resistant designs
  • Leverage international support through the Sudan Crisis Response Plan
  • Expand funding for disaster risk reduction initiatives

4. Policy & Financial Safeguards

Encouraging insurance coverage for critical infrastructure and households can mitigate financial losses. Regional cooperation with Ethiopia and Egypt on dam safety and seismic monitoring could prevent cascading disasters.

  • Promote earthquake insurance for infrastructure and households
  • Foster cross-border collaboration on seismic monitoring
  • Develop emergency response protocols for high-risk areas

Lessons from Global Practices

Countries like the United States, where cities such as Memphis (Tennessee) face similar moderate risks, offer valuable insights for Sudan.

Memphis, located near the New Madrid Seismic Zone, prioritizes earthquake-engineered infrastructure and public education. Sudan could emulate these strategies by:

  • Conducting vulnerability assessments for high-risk urban areas
  • Prioritizing retrofitting of hospitals, schools, and government buildings
  • Establishing emergency response protocols tailored to localized risks
  • Developing public education campaigns about earthquake preparedness

These measures have proven effective in reducing earthquake risks in regions with similar seismic profiles to Sudan.
